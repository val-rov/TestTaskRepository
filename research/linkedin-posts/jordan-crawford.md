## Every Cold Email Is the Same Template — And Why??

**Date:** Thu, 28 May 2026
**URL:** https://www.linkedin.com/posts/jordancrawford_stupid-compliment-3-sentenceexplainer-activity-7465784758124371969-PpjZ?utm_source=share&utm_medium=member_desktop&rcm=ACoAAFWF4egBCw1RxVdlCsF4TTgWYOsP4Y-eeyE

{{stupid compliment}} {{3 sentence explainer of you}} {{15 minute meeting request}} <— THIS IS EVERY COLD EMAIL AND WHY??

Look at how personal emails sound, in comparison to this…

1. Kris! you wanna get beers like Friday next week?

2. Where you at?!? I saw you at Blue Bottle the other day Mike Heller, who were you with? Let's hang.

3. Kyle Norton you should do a guest post on the edge, you down?

Notice how informal and direct these are… and you can do this too with cold email if you break from the template in your head.

Examples:

——
Subject: Get Joe
Did you file a permit for some new construction on Elm street in Austin last week Jessica?

A ton of our clients work in Austin, so I know the good permit inspectors from the slow ones… here are their names and approval times on average… honestly, just see if you can get Joe Turner for your project. He's the fastest.

{{names}}, {{approval times}}.

——
Subject: Chatting
{{screenshot of chat interaction on their site}}

I just had this chat with your bot, is this how it's supposed to work?

——
Subject: 100 places
Larry, I found everyone online who talked about Sedona more than 5 times in the last year and I put together my favorite Sedona things: {{link}}

There are a few you talked about that I have never been {{places he talked about}} should I add them… I can link to your post?

——

People are seeking novelty, they want humans, they want to know you're not a sales robot. They want to feel seen.

And we just sorta forgot that in B2B because we want the sale, we don't want to evoke an emotion.

But now, more than ever, we need to evoke an emotion. And we trained the robots to write like this hot garbage, they took the sales material online and they just ran with it.

Think like this first, "if my only goal was to share a real emotion, and evoke a real emotion in response… what would I say to THIS person"

This can be about your product too, btw, but often it's about the experience of the person in the seat…

"Ever just feel like when you get into an LLL you realize that the worst part about the experience is you? Ugh, I feel that way and wanted to know if you did too?"

You can build lists like that, but honestly, if someone DMs me and asks me a good deep question about any of my content, I always respond.

In a world of reckless, VC-backed, milk toast formatted sentences… the ones who will win are those who can pause, reflect, and connect to the person by relating their experiences with your experiences and leading with curiosity.

Don't burn in hell, let the robots do that, but you'll join them if you're mimicking how they talk.

## People Are Way Over Complicating AI in GTM

**Date:** Mon, 27 Apr 2026
**URL:** https://www.linkedin.com/posts/jordancrawford_people-are-way-over-complicating-aiin-gtm-activity-7454633354106302464-Yhbx?utm_source=share&utm_medium=member_desktop&rcm=ACoAAFWF4egBCw1RxVdlCsF4TTgWYOsP4Y-eeyE

People are way over complicating AI in GTM, it's only two things…

1. Better understanding of your customer (why they bought, why they churned, why they bought more, why they never bought). Using everything they ever said and everything they ever did. In both their words and their actions (not what's in the CRM). Plus intuition on "is this right?" And iterating until it is.

2. Once you know 1, all you need to do is find a way to make your imagination as big as possible and give the thing each customer wants the absolute most when they need it most.

All of this other stuff is really noise, and not based on a fundamental understanding of the customer.

In serving the customer with AI, you should also be serving your employees by replacing their model of the customer and related tasks with the AI's model WHERE APPROPRIATE (e.g. research is > customer communication from an implementation perspective.)

## I Built a Swarm Agent Claude Code Skill

**Date:** Thu, 26 Mar 2026
**URL:** https://www.linkedin.com/posts/jordancrawford_i-built-a-swarm-agent-claude-code-skill-thatactivity-7443074662214942721-X2Dj?utm_source=share&utm_medium=member_desktop&rcm=ACoAAFWF4egBCw1RxVdlCsF4TTgWYOsP4Y-eeyE

I built a swarm agent Claude Code skill that used 100% of my 4 hour context window in 20 mins… if you want it below comment your favorite cheese…

Here's what this skill does…

1. Profile the data — sample records, count tokens, find the garbage before it wastes context

2. Ask the real question — "why do customers churn?" not "analyze this data"

3. Test the prompt in the main window — 3-4 reps, destroy what doesn't work, keep what does

4. Optimize for token density — markdown tables, strip HTML, kill empty fields

5. Size the swarm — tokens per unit, units per agent, how many agents, which model tier

6. Show the plan, get approval — agents, cost estimate, assignments, output format. Nothing launches without a yes.

7. Fan out to visible Agent Teams — parallel Opus/Sonnet/Haiku subagents in tmux panes you can watch

8. Audit everything — dedicated Opus auditor cross-checks all outputs for fabrication and conflicts

9. Synthesize across batches — patterns that appear independently in multiple agents = high confidence

10. Iterate — a swarm isn't a one-shot, it's a conversation with your data at scale

The agent swarm lets you analyze thousands of records by testing your approach on a handful first, then fanning out to parallel Opus subagents you can watch work in real time — with a mandatory auditor that catches every fabrication before it hits your output. You never waste tokens on an unvalidated idea, and you never accept results without proof.
