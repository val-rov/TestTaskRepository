## One of the Most Useful Sales Lessons Doesn't Come From Sales

**Date:** Thu, 11 Jun 2026
**URL:** https://www.linkedin.com/posts/josh-braun_one-of-the-most-useful-sales-lessons-doesnt-activity-7470865129073520640-BsoU?utm_source=share&utm_medium=member_desktop&rcm=ACoAAFWF4egBCw1RxVdlCsF4TTgWYOsP4Y-eeyE

One of the most useful sales lessons doesn't come from sales.

It comes from psychologist George Loewenstein.

His research found that curiosity happens when people notice a gap between what they know and what they want to know. He called it the Information Gap.

Yet as sellers, we often fill the gaps.

We explain.
Pitch.
Educate.
Convince.

The problem?

People don't become curious because you gave them more information.

They become curious when they notice they're missing some.

Explaining sounds like this:

"We help nonprofits increase recurring giving by as much as 37% through our AI-powered donor engagement platform."

Compare that to:

"We're seeing that only 6.2% of donors give monthly. What are you currently doing to encourage one-time donors to become recurring donors?"

Now something different happens.

They start looking inward.
They compare.
They think.

Sometimes they discover a gap on their own.
And that's the moment curiosity appears.

The goal isn't to create a problem.
It's to help people notice one.

Great sellers don't fill information gaps.
They expose them.

Buyers have the answers.
Sellers have the questions.

Ditch the pitch.
Poke the bear.

## "The Price Is Higher Than We Budgeted For. I'm Not Sure We Can Make It Work."

**Date:** Thu, 11 Jun 2026
**URL:** https://www.linkedin.com/posts/josh-braun_the-price-is-higher-than-we-budgeted-for-activity7470814696447848448-BekS?utm_source=share&utm_medium=member_desktop&rcm=ACoAAFWF4egBCw1RxVdlCsF4TTgWYOsP4Y-eeyE

"The price is higher than we budgeted for. I'm not sure we can make it work."

You might reach for the discount to close by saying:

"Let me see what I can do."

Don't.

You just taught them your price is soft.

And if price wasn't the real issue, you'll cut margin.

And still lose the deal because whatever they're actually worried about is sitting there, untouched.

Try this instead:

"Sounds like price is your only concern."

Now watch what happens.

Either they confirm it, and you're negotiating from a place of truth or they flinch:

"Well… it's not just the price."

The sale stalls where the truth hasn't been told yet.

## When Someone Says "I Want to Think About It"

**Date:** Tue, 09 Jun 2026
**URL:** https://www.linkedin.com/posts/josh-braun_when-someone-says-i-want-to-think-about-activity7470089934922514432-TgS?utm_source=share&utm_medium=member_desktop&rcm=ACoAAFWF4egBCw1RxVdlCsF4TTgWYOsP4Y-eeyE

When someone says
"I want to think about it"

They're usually not thinking.

They're protecting themselves.

What it often really means:

I don't trust you yet.
I'm not sure you understand my situation.
I don't know if this would actually work here.
The price feels bigger than the problem.
Sticking with what I know feels safer than risking my job on something new.

Most people say "I want to think about it" because they don't want confrontation.
It's a polite exit.
Not a decision.

So how do you unlock the truth?

You don't push.
You don't explain.
You don't convince.

You take a guess, and then stop talking.

When someone says "I want to think about it," try one of these and then shut the front door.

"You're weighing whether this really fits your situation."

Silence.

"You're not fully convinced this would work in your environment."

Silence.

"You're protecting yourself from being the one who takes the risk."

Silence.

"The cost feels bigger than the problem right now."

Silence.

"You're deciding whether staying with what you know is safer than trying something new."

Silence.

Here's why this works.

You're not asking a question.
You're offering a testable hypothesis.

Their brain wants to correct you.

"Not exactly…"
"Yeah, but the real issue is…"

That correction is the truth.

And notice what you're not doing:

No "why."
No defense.
No justification.
No pitch.

You're letting them do the clarification work.

That's where the truth is.

## I Once Met a Salesperson Who Agreed With Everything I Said

**Date:** Mon, 08 Jun 2026
**URL:** https://www.linkedin.com/posts/josh-braun_i-once-met-a-salesperson-who-agreed-with-activity-7469727605345251329-7QI8?utm_source=share&utm_medium=member_desktop&rcm=ACoAAFWF4egBCw1RxVdlCsF4TTgWYOsP4Y-eeyE

I once met a salesperson who agreed with everything I said.

"That's a great question."
"Totally."

"I think X."
"Same here."

"Strawberry is the best ice cream flavor."
"Wow, me too."

At first, it felt friendly.
Then it started to feel… strategic.

When someone agrees with you on everything, trust erodes.
Not because agreement is bad.
But because perfect alignment feels manufactured.

Your brain starts asking:

Are they actually listening?
Or are they mirroring me so I'll like them?

A better move?

Disagree.

Not to argue.
Not to dominate.
But to show you're thinking independently.

For example:

Prospect:
"We're thinking of solving this internally."

You:
"How come?"
(Listen)
Then say: "One thing I've seen is engineers often get pulled into higher-priority fires. How are you protecting their time for this?"

Or:

Prospect:
"We're leaning toward the cheaper sofa."

You:
"Can I ask you question about the other sofa you're considering?"
"One common way furniture companies cut costs is cushion density."
"Density around 1.5 tends to last 1–2 years."
"Higher density, around 2.5 with coils, can last 10–15 years."
"Do you know the cushion density of the ACME sofa?"

Prospect:
"Hmm… I'm not sure."

That moment matters.

You're not trying to win the point.
You're not trying to sound smarter.

You're showing you're not afraid to have a perspective.

Paradoxically, thoughtful disagreement builds trust.

It signals:
I'm not here to flatter you.
I'm here to think with you.

People don't trust mirrors.
They trust minds.

## The $3 Umbrella vs. the $5 Umbrella

**Date:** Mon, 08 Jun 2026
**URL:** https://www.linkedin.com/posts/josh-braun_you-live-in-chicago-the-windy-city-activity7469707670783160321-CO2V?utm_source=share&utm_medium=member_desktop&rcm=ACoAAFWF4egBCw1RxVdlCsF4TTgWYOsP4Y-eeyE

You live in Chicago.

The Windy City,

Do you want the $3 umbrella or the $5 umbrella?

Most people choose the $3 umbrella.

If the umbrellas feel the same, of course you'll buy the cheaper one.

Now let me change the question.

Do you want the $3 umbrella…

Or the $5 umbrella with reinforced ribs that won't flip inside out, a vented canopy that handles 40 mph winds, and a lifetime replacement guarantee?

Suddenly, the $5 umbrella doesn't feel expensive.

Why?

Because it no longer looks the same.

When things appear interchangeable, people compare prices. When they don't, they compare tradeoffs.

Price is the last refuge when people don't see how your umbrella is meaningfully different.
