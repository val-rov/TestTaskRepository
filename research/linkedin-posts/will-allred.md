## I Turned a Founder Away From Buying Our Product

**Date:** Fri, 12 Jun 2026
**URL:** https://www.linkedin.com/posts/williamallred_i-turned-a-founder-away-from-buying-ourproduct-activity-7471167673960325122-3KSJ?utm_source=share&utm_medium=member_desktop&rcm=ACoAAFWF4egBCw1RxVdlCsF4TTgWYOsP4Y-eeyE

I turned a founder away from buying our product last week. Let me explain (bc I got this wrong too) why his poor understanding of CAC meant he should be focused on other channels first.

Context:
The product: $90/m
W/ 2 month free trial
Sells to influencer marketing

First off CAC = customer acquisition cost

Don't need immediate rev to cover CAC.

Just how long they need to be a customer (or how many purchases) for you to cover the CAC.

This is the payback period.

If you do a trial - you're extending the payback period.

But, if CAC is $90... you pay back in month 3 in this example.
0,0,$90

If the trial doubles conversions to paid, you lowered the CAC.

How?

Charge $90 in month 1, but it takes more $ spent to get them to convert without a trial.

Now... your CAC isn't going to be $90... it might be $270.

The payback period is still 3 months... but you gotta spend more to make it back. (more capital intensive = not good if bootstrapped!)

Most founders don't know this stuff because a lot of founders are folks that passionately want to solve a problem they experienced.

That was this founder. And like many just started doing 1:1 outreach to drum up business.

This is the same way we got our first 100 users. It's gritty work. It sucks. It must be done.

But… it doesn't scale and you need to evolve past it.

The experience of sending emails successfully says find a way to do more email… the math says "you sure?"

Discounting his existing success on email

Let's say 5% of emails get a positive reply (he was 35% - but scale hurts conversions)

And 33% start a trial

And then another 10% convert to paid

At $1.50 a contact in Ora, we're costing him

$30 per reply
$90 per trial
$900 per paying customer

That doesn't include the cost to service the account for free for 2 months.

But at $90 a month. They need to stick around for 10 months after trial to not lose money. They're current month to month contract strategy is going to fall apart.

There are cheaper ways to scale email.

But, should you try other channels like ads first?

Books like Traction are great resources because they teach you the power laws of growth efforts. Email in his "way" doesn't really scale. (longer story) So it's actually a new channel to scale email

Instead, he needs to test scaled email alongside other channels like ads to find his next outperforming channel.

He can be making long tail investments in things like content.

But, long tail doesn't pay a bootstrapper's bills today. It also doesn't satisfy growth goals from VCs (not his problem)

Let's say the ads convert for $45 a trial.

That payback period becomes 5 months instead of 10. But more importantly, he can get trial user data for less $ to start shoring up why folks churn out.

He still wants to try Ora... probably as a how do I pay this guy for the advice lol

But, also to test the math

Ora math might not work for everyone. That's a good thing :)

## Start With Why — And Why AI Messaging Fails That Test

**Date:** Wed, 10 Jun 2026 19:14:28 GMT
**URL:** https://www.linkedin.com/posts/williamallred_any-millennial-thats-worked-in-marketing-activity7470553996341129216-5Zsx?utm_source=share&utm_medium=member_desktop&rcm=ACoAAFWF4egBCw1RxVdlCsF4TTgWYOsP4Y-eeyE

Any millennial that's worked in marketing probably knows Simon Sinek's "start with why"... it also shouldn't be a stretch to realize Simon wouldn't approve of how AI is being marketed.

I'm guilty of the same Simon shame too... you're not alone

I've been drawing on the story telling lessons that I learned in my time consulting for brands like PBS, CEB, CenturyLink, E2Open, wealth management firms, libertarian think tanks, etc etc that I wanted to share.

Why? I think they'll help remove "AI" from your messaging

First... why does the industry have a problem?

We're so wrapped up in the "how"... aka the tech

More specifically the insecurities that stem from using the same LLMs as our competitors that show up as nuanced takes on why "our AI is different than the other guy"

it has made for a sea of undifferentiated noise

everyone assumes you're using AI... I'm here to tell you... that isn't the headline you think it is

Why... is hard with AI because no one wants to come in and say the problem is "people"... its f***ing bleak lol

"Why" has to be focused on the problems that your buyers and users are facing but also framed within your specific point of view (POV)

A perfect example (Jordan Crawford covered this in a post) is Clay.

They aren't selling an integration tool that uses AI.
They aren't marketing claygents and the reasons you shouldn't try to build one yourself

They're marketing turning you (the GTM engineer) into an AI hero within your org

It's not replacing people... it's unlocking your creativity

The POV that *you* need to be unlocked is unique

That point of view should be the differentiating factor. It's not tech... its your world view that says philosophically we see you and we're approaching this in a unique way.

Crafting that means you have to understand not only what they "think"... but also how they "feel".

We used to use a think, feel, act framework that unearthed some serious "a ha" moments when trying to craft brand narratives.

ex. its easy to stay logical with an AI email product... it does research, and sends personalized emails... cool everyone says this.

I want to tap how you *feel*... aka You buyers hate AI messaging. They won't hate ours.

That stance, allows you to open the door to explain how you do that.

Another ex. when scaling Lavender's chrome extension it wasn't the email coach... it was tapping into that uncomfortable feeling that you get when you don't have a second opinion on your copy.

As a user told us "Its embarrassing to ask for help... I went to college. I should know how to write an email."

That emotion is what drives us to act.

AI is irrelevant until the person on the other end feels like you truly understand them

This is the problem! We're pitching "AI this" and "AI that" without truly tapping into
- why something would be better with AI
- their fears about introducing AI
- what they gain from making change

There's logic here but so much is emotion

What other examples would you add?

## You Don't Go to the Olympics to Watch Cheetahs Race

**Date:** Wed, 10 Jun 2026 12:01:21 GMT
**URL:** https://www.linkedin.com/posts/williamallred_a-fun-quote-i-snagged-from-a-nytimes-oped-activity7470444998505680897-nFBh?utm_source=share&utm_medium=member_desktop&rcm=ACoAAFWF4egBCw1RxVdlCsF4TTgWYOsP4Y-eeyE

A fun quote I snagged from a NYTimes OpEd on AI:
"You don't go to the olympics to watch cheetahs race. You go to see the limits of human potential"

It's popular to talk about "AI replacement"

But, is that really a goal we should strive towards?

Shift perspective.

A horse and it's jockey
A F1 Driver and their car
A marathon runner and their shoes

Its not that AI is bad.
AI can replace things slowing us down.

But do we really want to see a world where commerce is an algorithm?
Do we want to see a world where your only colleagues are LLMs?

Of course not.

We want to see ourselves and our peers reach their greatest potential.
Build amazing things that solve problems.
Connect with our friends, peers and markets on a deeper, more thoughtful, level.

Anyways... loved the quote. Great reminder to keep the right goals in perspective :)

## The AI Talent Problem Is Actually an Architecture Problem

**Date:** Wed, 03 Jun 2026
**URL:** https://www.linkedin.com/posts/williamallred_a-lot-of-convos-about-needing-ai-talent-activity7467935529972670464-woVj?utm_source=share&utm_medium=member_desktop&rcm=ACoAAFWF4egBCw1RxVdlCsF4TTgWYOsP4Y-eeyE

A lot of convos about needing "ai" talent to help with internal ai projects. This is a deployment problem. Look at projects like Ramp Glass. 3 people enabled the entire company.

Problem 1: Tools

Tools like Claude cowork have a great 1 player mode (Minus some integrations being light/unusable) to get non-techies into it.

But there's no infra shared across the team. Everyone's set up is different so top users disproportionally get gains from the tech.

Someone in sales creates a better demo deck generator, that win is hard to scale.

So, that person is tagged to figure it out (they know ai right?)

They set up a workflow.

Then another.

Then another.

Team is winning right?

Problem 2: People

Next thing you know the AI guy/gal is swamped w requests.

CMO wants a deck generated.
RevOps wants help w a report.
CS wants a custom account monitor.

Your AI person is burnt.
Your AI progress is slowed.

AI person feels valuable. Ego is full. They're not going to fix it.

AI person gets better job offer.
*crap*

Solution: Better architecture

Instead of making the org reliant on individual workflow creation - build an internal system that makes it easy for anyone to build.

Ps.

If you're wrestling w this or feel like you need more "ai talent" I'm happy to be a sounding board.
