## A New Client Started With Us 3 Weeks Ago

**Date:** Thu, 11 Jun 2026
**URL:** https://www.linkedin.com/posts/outboundsales_a-new-client-started-with-us-3-weeks-ago-activity-7470822467968725011-Hv--?utm_source=share&utm_medium=member_desktop&rcm=ACoAAFWF4egBCw1RxVdlCsF4TTgWYOsP4Y-eeyE

A new client started with us 3 weeks ago.

They wanted to target 3 verticals:

Tech
Consulting
PE & VC Companies

We always start as broad as possible. Then niche down if we can't get the positive reply ratio we want.

We wait for at least 2k sends before making a decision.

In this case, consulting flopped. PE & VC did ok. But tech is converting one positive reply for every 362 leads. Pretty good for the first iteration.

Now, rather than try to improve results with consulting and pe/vc companies, we're going to double down on the vertical that's working: Tech companies.

Our plan is to niche down further (and make the messaging even more relevant, as a result) by adding persona based campaigns within tech companies.

If we can get the lead per positive reply rate down below 200, we'll scale it to the whole TAM.

This is how we think about testing. Hope this helps.

I ran the numbers on signal-based campaigns vs. standard campaigns recently and was surprised..

About 80% of our meetings come from campaigns with zero signals attached.

Signal-based outreach sounds appealing. And sometimes it works. But the lists are small, the setup takes longer, and you usually need extra tools (more $$) to pull it off.

IMO it's better to spend that time on finding a message that converts for a large audience - without much filtering/signals.

## My Favorite Cold Email Opener When I Don't Have a Signal: The Diagnostic Question

**Date:** Tue, 26 May 2026
**URL:** https://www.linkedin.com/posts/outboundsales_sales-activity-7465024444596486145-15qR?utm_source=share&utm_medium=member_desktop&rcm=ACoAAFWF4egBCw1RxVdlCsF4TTgWYOsP4Y-eeyE

My favorite way to open a cold email when I don't have a signal/personalization, is a "diagnostic question".

There's a thin line between one that makes someone pause and one that gets an eye roll.

The difference is almost always specificity.

Here are some examples of eye roll diagnostic questions:

- "Are you struggling to keep up with the competition?"
- "Do you have visibility into your pipeline?"

Everyone sees these in their inbox... all the time.

But here are good examples:
- "Do you have a way to see which of your open deals are actively evaluating a competitor right now?"
- "Can you tell, just from your CRM, which accounts are close to churning before they tell you?"

The eye roll versions could apply to anyone. The good versions describe something specific that actually makes the reader think.

If you're cold email campaigns aren't converting, stop worrying about fancy ai workflows and personalization.

Instead, focus on understanding your buyer better. What do they *actually* want? What are their *actual* process gaps?

The answers to those questions will unlock your best campaigns.

## The Cold Email Framework That Scales Forever

Most cold email frameworks have a ceiling because they rely on a limited segment of your market (job changers, funding rounds, etc.)

So it'll work for a little while, then the leads dry up and you have to find something new.

The framework I keep coming back to doesn't have this problem. It scales essentially forever because it's built on message quality, not signals.

It has 3 parts:

#1 - A diagnostic question that makes the reader stop and think "wait... do we actually have a way to do that?"

#2 - A specific, tangible thing they actually want

#3 - A disarming CTA that "breaks the 4th wall"

TBH this email only works if the diagnostic question is done right. And it usually takes me a lot of iterations to get there. Sometimes I don't even figure it out.

But when I do, it prints meetings.

## Sometimes It's Better to Let Your Emails Do the Filtering for You

**Date:** Fri, 08 May 2026
**URL:** https://www.linkedin.com/posts/outboundsales_sales-activity-7458501389934120960-oK4O?utm_source=share&utm_medium=member_desktop&rcm=ACoAAFWF4egBCw1RxVdlCsF4TTgWYOsP4Y-eeyE

Sometimes it's better to let your emails do the filtering for you.

We try to make our targeting as precise as possible. But some things you just can't filter for. No enrichment tool or AI prompt will reliably surface them.

Take budget for example. You can proxy for it: company size, funding stage, revenue range, but you can't actually know if someone has budget allocated for what you sell until you're in a conversation with them.

If you try to filter for something that abstract, you risk cutting a big chunk of your list that was probably qualified. You just couldn't confirm it ahead of time.

And honestly, is that worth it? If someone's outside your TAM, they won't reply. Or they'll tell you they're not interested. Neither of those is a big deal.

I'd rather have the full list and let the email do the work. The only exception is when your TAM is massive and you can genuinely afford to leave part of it out.
