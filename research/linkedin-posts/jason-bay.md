## 250+ Sales Teams. 10,000+ Reps. Here's the Best Cold Call Opener.

**Date:** Thu, 11 Jun 2026
**URL:** https://www.linkedin.com/posts/jasondbay_250-sales-teams-10000-reps-heres-theactivity-7470853434464989184-mCNH?utm_source=share&utm_medium=member_desktop&rcm=ACoAAFWF4egBCw1RxVdlCsF4TTgWYOsP4Y-eeyE

250+ sales teams. 10,000+ reps. Here's the best cold call opener…

We've studied hundreds of thousands of cold calls across 250+ sales teams in the last 6 years.

And there's one thing we can tell you for sure:

The best reps all use different openers. That's right. There's no one opener. Or handful of openers that work the best.

BUT…here's what every great opener that led to a meeting had in common.

The opener has three parts:

Part 1) A greeting

"Hi [first name], it's [your first name…"

They answer the first question every prospect has: who are you?

Part 2) The three Ts

Here's where it starts to get interesting. Each of the openers leverages one of what we call the "three Ts":

Trigger: Make it timely by finding signals/triggers
Tension: Make it relevant by sharing a problem
Trust: Make it credible by leveraging social proof

Sometimes they include all three. But every time, they include at least one.

"Hi [first name], it's [your first name…saw you're hiring welders…"

That's an example of leveraging a trigger.

Part 3) Permission

Gong data from 300k+ cold calls supports the same. Permission-based openers outperform any other opener by nearly 5x.

"Hi [first name], it's [your first name…saw you're hiring welders…have a minute for me to share why I called you specifically?"

A simple permission-based question.

~~~

Now here's what it looks like when you start to get creative:

Greeting + Tension + Permission: "Hi Dave, was calling about the welder shortage everyone's dealing with right now. Got a minute for me to share more?"

Greeting + Trigger + Tension + Trust + Permission: "Hi Kate, noticed you're hiring MIG welders, not sure if you're running into challenges automating some of those low volume welds folks like Caterpillar have had. Got a min? Promise to be brief."

Greeting + Trust + Permission: "Hi Tom, we work with several trailer hitch manufacturers like XYZ. It's Jason with ABC COMPANY. Heard our name tossed around?" (h/t to Armand Farrokh)

~~~

The best cold call openers are the ones you create. Trigger, tension, trust is just the ingredients. You make your own recipe.

## Not All Humans Are Fit for Outbound

**Date:** Tue, 09 Jun 2026
**URL:** https://www.linkedin.com/posts/jasondbay_not-all-humans-are-fit-for-outbound-tara-activity7470127581493825537-wSb7?utm_source=share&utm_medium=member_desktop&rcm=ACoAAFWF4egBCw1RxVdlCsF4TTgWYOsP4Y-eeyE

Not all humans are fit for outbound

Tara DiCristo-Schmitt is the CRO at Houzz. She also spent 5 years as a VP of Sales at HubSpot and started her sales career at ADP.

Tara has a hot take I can't stop thinking about (and couldn't agree with more).

Stop trying to convert your inbound team to an outbound team.

This is the request we get more than any other. "We've been an inbound shop. Pipeline's down. We need to flip the team."

Keep your inbound team intact. Or in the AI era, reduce headcount. That's revenue you can depend on. Don't break the thing that works.

For outbound, hire net new. Build the army separately, with people who actually want to do the work.

You can teach skill. You cannot teach will. A generation of reps joined inbound shops because they were promised they wouldn't have to cold call. You can run the best enablement program in the world, and most of them still won't cut it.

Every "we tried mandatory call blocks and it didn't work" story starts with a team hired for one motion and asked to do another.

## The First Line of a Cold Email Is Not Just to Get Them to Read the Second Line

**Date:** Fri, 05 Jun 2026
**URL:** https://www.linkedin.com/posts/jasondbay_i-disagree-that-the-first-line-of-a-cold-activity7468678070321770497-EtBb?utm_source=share&utm_medium=member_desktop&rcm=ACoAAFWF4egBCw1RxVdlCsF4TTgWYOsP4Y-eeyE

I disagree that the first line of a cold email is to get them to read the second line.

That's not how most people actually read written content.

Google, Nielsen, CMSWire, EyeQuant...they've all done studies over the years.

And the conclusion is this:

- People read in either an F shape
- OR people read just the left-hand side of the page
- 2/3 of people skip the intro and jump straight to the end
- The middle body is the most overlooked part of the content

When I closely pay attention to my own habits, I open the email, read the first line, then immediately skip to the CTA to see what the ask is.

So what's the objective of the first line?

Two parts:

1) Drive the open
2) Build urgency

The first line of the email shows up in the preview text before the prospect ever opens it. To drive opens, it's just as important as the subject line.

Next, the first line should build urgency by talking about something hyper-relevant to the buyer. Something going on with their business, a challenge across their industry, a relevant competitor, etc

Here are three options, using an HR solution as an example:

Trigger opener

Open with a trigger that helps answer the "why now?" question for the buyer.
"Saw you just opened locations in Italy and France..."

Tension

Open with a problem you suspect they might have, or that's prevalent across their industry.
"International hiring can create a ton of compliance challenges that slows down hiring."

Trust

Open with a social proof statement.
"Not sure if you're running into this, but ABC COMPANY ran into tons of compliance challenges that slowed down hiring when they went international for the first time."

~~~

Let's not kid ourselves. Buyers are rarely reading our entire email the first time around.

Give them a reason to open.

Then give them a reason to pay attention.

Leverage triggers, tension statements, and trust statements.

## They Put Me on the Spot: Cold Calling for a Product I Don't Sell

**Date:** Tue, 02 Jun 2026
**URL:** https://www.linkedin.com/posts/jasondbay_they-put-me-on-the-spot-cold-calling-foractivity-7467604973116129281-82R-?utm_source=share&utm_medium=member_desktop&rcm=ACoAAFWF4egBCw1RxVdlCsF4TTgWYOsP4Y-eeyE

They put me on the spot. Cold calling for a product I don't sell. 5 minutes to build a script. And only 5 connects to book a meeting.

There's nothing worse than a "sales trainer" teaching cold calls who doesn't know how to do it themselves, am I right?

30 Minutes to President's Club put me to the test.

Here was my approach:

The opener: nailing the first 30 seconds

I need to grab the buyer's attention in a unique way.

Every great opener has a strong Reason To Meet (RTM):

- Trigger: Why should I engage now?
- Tension: Why should I consider change?
- Trust: Why should I continue talking with you?

Not necessarily in that order. And not all at once.

I like the initial opener to have the trigger. It might sound like this:

"Hi Dave, saw you're hiring AEs right now. It's Jason with Outbound Squad. Can I share why I'm calling you specifically?"

Bonus points if you can include more than one of the Ts in the opener like this:

"Hi Dave, saw you're hiring AEs right now. It's Jason with Outbound Squad. Our clients like Shopify are very focused on making sure those AEs can self-source their own pipeline right out of onboarding, which we help support. Have a minute for me to share more about why I'm calling?"

Trigger, tension, trust...the entire RTM all in the opener there.

The tension

Next, I need to really understand the buyer's problem. The tension they're feeling on a daily basis that makes them take action.

My approach here is always to "chunk up"

We have two types of tension:

- Strategic tension: problems that affect the business (execs care more about this)
- Surface tension: problems that affect the day-to-day for those executing the work

If I'm calling execs, I want to focus on strategic tension and how problems affect revenue and profit

~~~

Spoiler: I had a 20% conversion rate into a meeting for a product I do not sell.

## The "Do More With Less" Value Prop Is Biting SaaS Companies Right Now

**Date:** Mon, 01 Jun 2026
**URL:** https://www.linkedin.com/posts/jasondbay_the-do-more-with-less-value-prop-is-bitingactivity-7467228481160908800-qcQe?utm_source=share&utm_medium=member_desktop&rcm=ACoAAFWF4egBCw1RxVdlCsF4TTgWYOsP4Y-eeyE

The "do more with less" value prop is biting a lot of software companies in the a** right now.

SaaS: "We'll help your [insert user persona] be 20% more efficient."

Customer: "That worked great! Let's reduce headcount by 20%."

SaaS: "But wait, it's renewal time, and we need to sell more seats."

Customer: "Well, we want the same per-seat pricing but for 20% fewer seats."

Sound familiar?

Which begs the question...is user-based pricing dying?

We've worked with 250+ SaaS teams in the last 5-6 years. Here are the different models we've seen, plus our predictions below.

- Revenue share: Shopify's a great example. When their clients win, they continue to win.
- Flat/fixed: Basecamp is famous for this. One price, unlimited access regardless of size.
- Usage-based: The credit model also fits into here. All the LLMs use this model. You pay for what you use.
- Performance-based: Intercom Fin is charging $.99 per resolution. It's based on the completed task or outcome.
- User-based: Sell more seats. The CRMs popularized this model.

~~~

In an era where so much of the software does the same thing, the pricing model can be a true differentiator.

I predict that we'll see a ton of SaaS move towards usage and performance-based models. Their customers will demand it. And they need to do it to survive.

Right now, the goals are not aligned well:

SaaS: Sell more seats
Customer: Get a positive ROI

Those goals need to be more aligned in 2026 and beyond. We are no longer in an era where customers will accept paying loads for software that doesn't get the result they want.

## Are We Really Still Starting Emails With "I Trust This Finds You Well"?

**Date:** Tue, 26 May 2026
**URL:** https://www.linkedin.com/posts/jasondbay_are-we-really-still-starting-emails-withactivity-7465086532509405184-yqhN?utm_source=share&utm_medium=member_desktop&rcm=ACoAAFWF4egBCw1RxVdlCsF4TTgWYOsP4Y-eeyE

Are we really still starting emails with "I trust this finds you well..."?

Remember what you're competing against when you cold email prospects.

Most cold emails start like this…

- "I was hoping…"
- "My name is…"
- "I hope this email finds you…"
- "I was reaching out because…"
- "I was following up…"
- "I can imagine…"

It's all about the salesperson.

Try doing the opposite by opening your emails with:

- "Your _____"
- "What you _____"
- "Love your _____"
- "_____ was great…"
- "Does your _____"
- "Saw that _____"

Pack something relevant right into the first 3-5 words.

1) Be relevant
2) Be timely

The bar is low everyone!
